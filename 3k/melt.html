  1.  
    [intransitive, transitive] to become or make something become liquid as a result of heating
    • The snow showed no sign of melting.
    • melting ice
    • melt something The sun had melted the snow.
    • boiled potatoes with melted butter
    • melted cheese/chocolate/ice cream
    • First, melt two ounces of butter.

  2. [intransitive, transitive] to become or to make a feeling, an emotion, etc. become gentler and less strong
    • The tension in the room began to melt.
    • melt something Her trusting smile melted his heart.
  3. (of a person) to become extremely hot
    • Today the temperature got up to 36 degrees and it felt like I was melting.
    4. Word OriginOld English meltan, mieltan, of Germanic origin; related to Old Norse melta ‘to malt, digest’, from an Indo-European root shared by Greek meldein ‘to melt’, Latin mollis ‘soft’, also by malt.
Idioms
butter wouldn’t melt (in somebody’s mouth)
  1. (informal) used to say that somebody seems to be innocent, kind, etc. when they are not really
melt in your mouth
  1. (of food) to be soft and very good to eat
    • They serve steaks that just melt in your mouth.
